# Books

I enjoy reading! You can find my reviews and books on [Goodreads](https://www.goodreads.com/user/show/115083557-charles).

## 2026

- **Technopoly: The Surrender of Culture to Technology** by Neil Postman — The value of reading original canon, like Technopoly, is you skip the discourse and go straight to the origin. So many twitter takes and anecdotes that form tech discourse today are merely poor riffs on ideas Postman presented 30 years ago. And of course, Postman himself was merely a vessel of McLuhan, Ong, Illich, and others! It was particularly interesting to read Technopoly after "After Virtue" by MacIntyre, who come from totally different fields but arrive at similar diagnoses about our societal woes. The death of social institutions as information control mechanisms, the need for narrative and meaning in historical education, are all conclusions MacIntyre would have been familiar with. The first half of the book is incredibly compelling, dense in anecdotes and frameworks that are still relevant today. Second half unfortunately falls into common tropes of tech criticism that was much less interesting e.g. stethescopes are bad. Postman concludes with a vision for humanist education. While not particularly distinctive, it strikes me that education continues to reappear as the actual solution to many societal problems, a la Rickover.

- **The Paper Menagerie and Other Stories** by Ken Liu

- **The Disappearance of Rituals: A Topology of the Present** by Byung-Chul Han — A powerful book. While I don't agree with Han's framing in every chapter, particularly an overuse of the word "neoliberal", he offers an important and fresh perspective on the disappearance of rituals in every part of our life: death, celebrations, sex, war, etc. A must-read for anyone trying to make sense of the new age we live in, and thankfully quite brief (arguably — too brief and left me wanting more exposition in each chapter).

- **Dare We Hope That All Men Be Saved?** by Hans Urs von Balthasar — This book poses an interesting question — one that's somewhat frowned upon in the theological milieu I'm in. Still, I always find it valuable to see how different traditions interpret and emphasize particular verses, and this book offered a helpful exploration in that regard e.g. 1 Timothy 2:4, John 12:32, Matthew 18:30. That said, I found it a bit too academic in both form and presentation. The book references an overwhelming number of verses (some of which I would interpret differently) and spends much of its time debating other theologians. There's definitely an audience that would appreciate engaging with this level of rigor and discourse; I'm just not yet far enough along in my own readings to be part of it. Thanks to S.R. for the recommendation!

## 2025

- **Fear and Trembling** by Soren Kierkegaard — Many people have recommended Kierkegaard to me over the years. I have finally arrived to one of his classics and it did not disappoint! In Fear and Trembling, Kierkegaard takes a well known bible story of Abraham and Isaac, and questions its true meaning and what it says about the nature of faith. Even today, it feels like there are so many calls to "go beyond faith" to the intellectual or to the political. But who can understand the faith of a man and the duties it comes with? Particular points that resonated with me: the interiority of obligations of faith which no one can understand (indeed, the mystery of it — a faith that leads in to the unknown!); the method of deep meditation on Abraham and his motivations, taking seriously, more seriously than anyone, the actual stories of the bible and trying to understand what it means.

- **India after Gandhi: A History** by Ramachandra Guha — A magisterial book that fulfills the titular promise — it does indeed provide a comprehensive History of India after Gandhi. Despite its 750+ page length, the digestible sections and chapters never made it tiresome. A few things that surprised me about India: how contingent the union was; the deep spiritual forces within India; the incredible diversity of India.

- **Breakneck: China's Quest to Engineer the Future** by Dan Wang — The new China 101 primer book! Review forthcoming in American Affairs Fall 2025.

- **Freedom's Forge** by Arthur Herman — This book makes two important contributions: First, it gives a quantitative sense of the sheer scale of American manufacturing output and the incredible pace of conversion to wartime production in early WWII. Second, it introduces the idea that private industry leaders can play a critical role in supporting U.S. national power. Despite this, it also comes with several shortcomings including lack of editorial oversight and an overt ideological message against unions and Keynesian government intervention.

- **The Diffusion of Military Power** by Michael Horowitz — As an institutionalist, I very much enjoyed Horowitz's contribution to how militaries adopt technology innovation as dictated by organizational capacity and financial intensity. The introduction contains the key theory + 4 chapters of case studies (battleship, suicide warfare, aircraft carrier, nuclear weapons).

- **Orthodoxy** by G.K. Chesterton — A delightful read where GK Chesterton walks through his thoughtful journey to faith. It is also striking that a book written over a 100 years ago is still so relevant today. Filled with pithy quotes and scathing remarks that make for an entertaining read.

- **Babel** by R.F. Kuang — Finished this book in 36 hours! The magic that animates this world is the tension between languages, between cultures, between worlds of understanding. To see the same tensions I've felt in my American immigrant experience weaved into the fantasy magic of a book was a unique experience.

- **Showdown at Gucci Gulch** by Jeffrey Birnbaum — An incredibly in-depth account of how the 1986 tax reform bill came to pass. Particularly relevant as Inflation Reduction Act defense is playing out in real time today.

- **Orality and Literacy: The Technologizing of the Word** by Walter J. Ong — Ong gives an incredible anthology of what a truly oral culture is like. Particularly timely to read while going through apologetics again and thinking about the historicity of the gospel texts.

- **The Case for Christ** by Lee Strobel — Finally got around to this recommended apologetics classic. The interview/narrative style was fairly engaging. Unfortunately, the style was a bit too pop/casual.

- **The Brothers Karamazov** by Fyodor Dostoevsky — A friend recently asked me what Brothers Karamazov is about. In a word: life. Within its pages, you'll find every kind of person you've ever met, their thoughts and character rendered with remarkable depth. If you want to read a book about life in all its messy, profound complexity, there's none better than The Brothers Karamazov.

## 2024

- **A Defense Of Prejudice And Other Essays** by John Grier Hibben — A provocative series of essays, all thematically focused on describing a specific mindset and way of seeing the world. The perspectives draw from a unique mix of christian convictions, forceful agency, and drive for progress.

- **Deng Xiaoping and the Transformation of China** by Ezra F. Vogel — A rich history of a generational leader who set China on its trajectory today. Many of Deng's core convictions — science & technology as key drivers of economic development, policy experimentation as ground truth, and the central importance of the party to the nation — are still true of China's policymaking today.

- **Till We Have Faces** by C.S. Lewis — This retelling of Cupid and Psyche in many ways feels like the novel that encapsulates the archetypes and convictions that appear most often across CS Lewis's writing.

- **Department of Energy, 1977–1994: A Summary History** by Terrence R. Fehner & Jack M. Holl — A somewhat dry but still interesting history of how the mission, culture, and organization of the agency that is now the Department of Energy came to be.

- **The Singapore Story: Memoirs of Lee Kuan Yew** by Lee Kuan Yew — An incredibly detailed look into 20th century Singapore and how decolonialism unfolded in this unique part of the Indo-Pacific. LKY was truly a remarkable, generational leader.

- **Notes from Underground** by Fyodor Dostoevsky — Another classic exploration of the human condition. Themes: the rage and frustration that impotence and lack of agency can foment; the subjugation of the intellect to irrational emotion.

## 2023

- **Master of the Senate (The Years of Lyndon Johnson, #3)** by Robert A. Caro — LBJ's story captures a tension we still discuss today: is it better to be idealistic or is it better to be effective?

- **Pieces of the Action** by Vannevar Bush — An authentic first person account of how political personas during WWII shaped defense funding and science policy. Kudos to Stripe Press for bringing another lost classic back to print.

- **Counterfeit Gods** by Timothy J. Keller — Another Tim Keller classic and a timely reminder on what truly matters.

- **Making Climate Policy Work** by Danny Cullenward — A great interdisciplinary analysis of why economically optimal policies are not necessarily the ones that pass or actually work in practice.

- **The Rickover Effect: How One Man Made a Difference** by Theodore Rockwell — An incredible historical overview of a time when it felt like we could do anything, where one office in the US government was singlehandedly inventing new materials and building from scratch the industry needed to mass produce nuclear reactors and submarines.

- **21st Century Monetary Policy** by Ben S. Bernanke — A good primer on how central banks drive monetary policy and affect the economy and trade.

- **The Long Game: China's Grand Strategy to Displace American Order** by Rush Doshi — Doshi provides a clear historical argument for the China hawk position.

- **The Quest: Energy, Security, and the Remaking of the Modern World** by Daniel Yergin — An incredibly detailed overview of the history of energy infrastructure, as well as the people behind it.

- **Means of Ascent (The Years of Lyndon Johnson, #2)** by Robert A. Caro — For me, this book is unexpectedly the story of Coke Stevenson. Caro's book provides an incredible review of the tactics used by power-hungry men.

- **Working: People Talk About What They Do All Day** by Studs Terkel — Terkel's ethnography of the everyday worker in the 1980's elides simple narratives about work.

- **A Brief Guide to Maritime Strategy** by James R. Holmes — Exactly what was promised: a brief and introductory overview to the theory of naval grand strategy.

## 2022

- **Justice: A Reader** by Michael J. Sandel — Good mix of ancient/classics with modern day applications.

- **The Rivers North of the Future: The Testament of Ivan Illich** by David Cayley — A broad ranging interview with Ivan Illich. Perhaps one of the most influential Christian books I have read yet.

- **The Color of Law** by Richard Rothstein — A great history of how zoning and WWII federal housing financing was used to segregate and economically disadvantage African Americans.

- **Neighborhood Defenders** by Katherine Levine Einstein — How local zoning boards are a battleground for building more housing.

- **Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital** by Carlota Pérez — Perez's insight into technological development is that innovation does not seamlessly integrate with our society.

- **Games: Agency As Art** by C. Thi Nguyen — His basic thesis is that games are a vehicle for exploring different modes of agency.

- **Christian Theology** by Millard J. Erickson — Read for Gordon Conwell's introductory theology classes.

- **Christian Theology: An Introduction** by Alister E. McGrath — Read for Gordon Conwell's introductory theology classes.

- **Majority World Theology** by Gene L. Green — Interesting overview of how non-western cultures interact with Christian theology.

- **The End of History and the Last Man** by Francis Fukuyama — Fukuyama's work is far more nuanced than its commentators give him credit for. The last 3 sections on the "Last Man" warn that universal, egalitarian liberalism will lead to its own discontentment.

- **The Path to Power (The Years of Lyndon Johnson, #1)** by Robert A. Caro — Caro redefines the biography genre with this 750 page epic on LBJ's childhood and early career.

- **Debt: The First 5,000 Years** by David Graeber — A less pithy subtitle could have been "an Anthropologist tells economists what they missed." Chock-full of cultural studies and descriptions of societies as they span the ages.

- **Administrative Burden: Policymaking by Other Means** by Pamela Herd — Good overview of administrative burdens, an often under-considered dimension of policy making.

- **Life in the Trinity** by Donald Fairbairn — A refreshing examination of the early church fathers and how they conceptualized the Holy Trinity.

- **Asian Americans and the Spirit of Racial Capitalism** by Jonathan Tran — A compelling argument for the limits of identitarian antiracism.

- **The Gospel Comes with a House Key** by Rosaria Champagne Butterfield — A compelling vision of what Christian hospitality actually looks like.

- **Crying in H Mart** by Michelle Zauner — I never fully appreciated the role food plays as an anchor for the often displacing experience of being a second generation immigrant kid growing up in America.

- **Life Together** by Dietrich Bonhoeffer — Lots of great insight about Christian community and the dangers that come with any social group.
